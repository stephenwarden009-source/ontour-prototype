Major tours are announced. Fans rush online. Many miss out. Then come the rage-posts, the bad press, the lawsuits, the Senate hearings. The problem is not the system. The problem is that fans are in the dark about the realities of a complicated business. The industry has a messaging problem. onTour publishes what industry insiders understand and see — real demand data, realistic odds, what the show costs to build and run, historical numbers, and just why tickets to top tours are increasingly treasured and frustratingly hard to obtain. Most fans don't read Pollstar or Billboard. onTour fills that gap. And here's something those publications know that fans don't: most shows and tours don't sell out. Insatiable demand is a superstar issue. For fans, each of their shows is like the Super Bowl or F1 rolling into town — and have you seen what those tickets go for?

What onTour is for
For fans: You already know the concert ticketing experience is frustrating. Queues that move like molasses. Pinballing prices. The feeling that bots and scalpers got there first and you never had a real chance. Most of that frustration comes not from the system being broken — but from having no information about what you were walking into. onTour publishes that information: how many seats, how many people want them, what it cost to build the show, and what your real options are. Before the sale opens. Every time. Most fans don't read Pollstar or Billboard — but they deserve the same clarity that industry insiders take for granted. onTour fills that gap.
For the industry: The live music industry is navigating the most difficult reputational period in its history — Senate hearings, FTC suits, a monopoly verdict, and a sustained media narrative that frames every record-setting tour as evidence of exploitation. Most of that narrative is driven by fan fury that stems from a simple information gap. onTour closes that gap. Proactive transparency — publishing what the industry already knows — is not a concession to critics. It is the most effective response available to an industry that has, until now, chosen silence.
The thesis behind onTour was first published in Pollstar, April 2023, by Steve Warden: the concert ticketing industry has an information problem as much as a supply problem. Transparency — real numbers, plainly stated — is more useful to fans than outrage, and better for the industry than silence.
On scarcity: Scarcity is a proven strategy in the business of luxury goods — from fine wines to handbags to watches to high-performance automobiles. In the case of concert tickets, the scarcity is not deliberate. It is a function of a handful of superstar artists having literally billions of fans who want to attend a relatively tiny number of live shows. The math is the math. The concert industry has treated that math as an embarrassing problem to manage rather than an honest fact to explain. onTour explains it.
On keeping fans coming back: onTour is not a one-time announcement tool. As a tour progresses — added dates, new markets, ticket release windows, presale codes, day-of availability — that information belongs on a living page that fans can return to. The goal is to become the place fans check first when a tour is announced, and keep checking throughout the run.
The Live Nation cooperation case
Live Nation posted $25.2B in 2025 revenue, hosted 159M fans, and has already booked 80%+ of its 2026 large-venue calendar. It also faces a monopoly verdict, FTC suit, DOJ settlement, $280M payout, and an internal executive caught on record boasting about "robbing fans blind." In that context, a tool that publishes honest supply/demand math at every announcement changes the narrative from "price-gouging monopolist" to "the company that finally tells fans the truth." That is worth more than any legal settlement.
